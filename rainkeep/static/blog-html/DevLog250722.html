DevLog 250722 — Nomachine Kitchen Table Workstation

> Log Date: 2025-07-22

After rebuilding my MCP system from scratch, I now have a GPU-powered workstation accessible from the kitchen table thanks to Nomachine, turning everyday downtime into development time.

It took a lot of patience to get here. After my MCP came online, I experienced a complete system crash that forced a wipe and reinstall. The upside? I used the opportunity to set up a much cleaner environment: a fresh Ubuntu 24 install, tighter directory structures, and a better desktop streaming solution. I'm now using Nomachine to remote into my GPU tower from both my old MacBook Air and my iPad—and it's surprisingly smooth.

Why Nomachine Worked

I experimented with Sunshine and Moonlight, but Moonlight had macOS 12 compatibility issues, and other tools failed to give me the right blend of latency and quality. Nomachine, however, just worked. It streams a full remote desktop over LAN or WAN, supports hardware-accelerated encoding, and includes clipboard and file sharing between devices.

I installed the Nomachine server on my Ubuntu tower and downloaded the clients to macOS and iPad. From there, I just needed to open port 4000 on my router, set a static LAN IP, and connect with the credentials. Once I was in, the latency was low enough to scrub timelines in Blender—a huge win.

Implications for Workflow

This feels small on the surface, but the shift is monumental. I now have GPU access from the kitchen while checking downloads between loads of laundry. This means more usable hours in the day. I can brush up on Blender modeling and scene-building, or queue up batch jobs on Whisper or AnimateDiff, all without being tethered to the desk.

This also sets up my iPad + Apple Pencil as a Blender sculpting station, something I’ve wanted for a long time. It makes the entire 3D print and animation stack accessible across devices without disrupting life’s flow. The development environment now matches the rhythm of the household.
